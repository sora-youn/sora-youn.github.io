The screen which we use for asking for your estimates looks like this:

There are 10 bins in each of Box A (the upper box) and Box B (the bottom box). To provide an estimate concerning the probability that you received a certain number of Green balls, you are asked to distribute 100 tokens, by typing an integer ranging from 0 to 100, into the 10 bins in Box A. To provide an estimate concerning the probability that your teammate received a certain number of Green balls, you are asked to distribute another 100 tokens, by typing an integer ranging from 0 to 100, into the 10 bins in Box B.

Each bin represents a certain quantity of Green balls you (Box A) and your teammate (Box B) received. Each token represents one percentage point. Therefore, the number of tokens you distribute to each bin in Box A represents your belief about the percent chance that you received a certain quantity of green balls. The number of tokens you distribute to each bin in Box B represents your belief about the percent chance that your teammate received a certain quantity of green balls. The more likely you deem a certain bin to represent the number of Green balls you and your teammate received, the more tokens should be put into this bin.

Here is an example of how it works.

  • Suppose you have distributed tokens as shown in the above screenshot.
  • You have placed 50 tokens in each of the “20” bin and the “30” bin, and 0 tokens in all the other bins in Box A. This implies that you assume that with a 50% probability you received 20 Green balls and with a 50% probability you received between 30 Green balls.
  • You have placed 77 tokens in the “80” bin, 23 tokens in the “90” bin, and 0 tokens in all the other bins in Box B. This implies that with a 77% probability you assume that your teammate received 80 Green balls and with a 23% probability you assume that your teammate received between 90 Green balls.
  • This is just an example to illustrate. The chances you have in mind may be completely different.

The task will only be finished when you have distributed exactly 100 tokens into the 10 bins in each of Box A and Box B. In other words, the last column "# of tokens distributed so far" in each box must be 100 to move on to Part 3. If you feel confident about the resulting probability distributions because they are good fits to your estimates concerning the numbers of Green balls you and your teammate received, please press the “Confirm” button to continue with Part 3 of the experiment.

Now, we explain how your payoff will be determined.
The bottom line is that the more accurate your estimate is, the more likely you will earn a higher payoff.

 

How is your payoff determined?

Step 1: At the end of Part 2, your penalty score for each of your estimates will be calculated in the following way. You will receive a higher penalty score from Box A as your estimate concering yourself deviates more from the actual number of Green balls you received. You will receive a higher penalty score from Box B as your estimate concerning your teammate deviates more from the actual number of Green balls your teammate received.



Step 2: Then, a number XA is drawn randomly from the interval [0,20000]. Any number within the interval [0,20000] is equally likely to be selected. Independent of XA, a number XB is also drawn in the same way. If your Penalty Score from Box A is less than or equal to the random number XA, you will earn 30 Experimental Dollars. Otherwise, you will earn zero. If your Penalty Score from Box B is less than or equal to the random number XB, you will earn 30 Experimental Dollars. Otherwise, you will earn zero. In other words, the higher penalty score you get, the lower probability it is that you earn 30 Experimental Dollars for each of your estimates.

 

To help your understanding, suppose you received 60 Green balls. Notice that, in this example, the bin that represents the actual number of Green balls you received is the bin "60".

    You feel very confident that you received 60 Green balls. Therefore, you allocated all the 100 tokens to the “60” bin. If this is the case, you receive no penalty because your estimate is very accurate. You earn 30 Experimental Dollars with certainty (100 percent chance).

Part 3 consists of {{num_game_round}} rounds.

In Part 3, you are presented with two urns. Each urn contains a total of 200 balls, each of which is Green or Black.

  • Out of 200 balls in Urn 1, there are as many Green balls as YOU and YOUR TEAMMATE received in Part 1. The rest of the balls are Black.
  • Out of 200 balls in Urn 2, There are 40 Green balls. The rest of the balls are Black.

In every round, you are asked to draw a total of 100 balls from Urn 1 and Urn 2 with replacement. "With replacement" means that once you draw a ball from one of the two urns, you put the ball back in the urn where it was drawn before you draw another ball. Drawing with replacement makes the probabilities of drawing a Green ball (and the probabilities of drawing a Black ball) from Urn 1 and Urn 2 constant.


Here are some examples:

  • Suppose you received 30 Green balls and your teammate received 60 Green balls in Part 1.
    This means, whenever a ball is drawn from Urn 1, the ball is Green with (30+60)/2 = 45 percent chance and Black with the remaining percent chance (100-45 = 55 percent chance). Furthermore, whenever a ball is drawn from Urn 2, the ball is Green with 40/2 = 20 percent chance and Black with the remaining percent chance (100-20 = 80 percent chance).
  • Suppose you received 50 Green balls and your teammate received 25 Green balls in Part 1.
    This means, whenever a ball is drawn from Urn 1, the ball is Green with (50+25)/2 = 37.5 percent chance and Black with the remaining percent chance (100-37.5 = 62.5 percent chance). Furthermore, whenever a ball is drawn from Urn 2, the ball is Green with 40/2 = 20 percent chance and Black with the remaining percent chance (100-20 = 80 percent chance).
  • These are just examples for your clarification. The chances you will face in the actual experiment may be completely different.

Remember that you and your teammate received Green balls based on the performance of the slider task in Part 1. The better YOU were at the slider task, the more Green balls you received, and hence the more Green balls are in Urn 1. The better YOUR TEAMMATE was at the slider task, the more Green balls your teammate received, and hence the more Green balls are in Urn 1.


Your task in Part 3 is to decide how many balls to draw from each urn. You can draw 1-99 balls from a single urn.

In every round, you have a chance to win the prize of {{prize}} points. The more lottery tickets you get, the more likely you win the prize. We explain how you get lottery tickets in the next page.

At the end of every round, the computer draws a random number "X" from the interval [0,{{max_lt}}]. Any number within the interval [0,{{max_lt}}] is equally likely to be selected.   • If you've got 0 lottery tickets, there is no chance that you win the prize (0/25).
  • If you've got 10 lottery ticket, you win the prize with 40 percent chance (10/25).
  • If you've got 20 lottery ticket, you win the prize with 80 percent chance (20/25).
  • If you've got 25 lottery tickets or more than 25 lottery tickets, you win the prize with 100 percent chance (25/25).

How do you get lottery tickets?

There are two factors that determine how many lottery tickets you will get:

(1) How many Green balls are drawn.

For every drawn Green ball, you have a chance to get a lottery ticket. However, there is no chance that a drawn Black ball awards you a lottery ticket.
Therefore, you are likely to get more lottery tickets as you draw more Green balls.

(2) How many balls you decide to draw from each urn

The probability of a drawn Green ball awarding you a lottery ticket depends on how many balls you decided to draw from each urns. The more balls you decided to draw from a single urn, the less likely it is that a Green ball drawn from that urn awards you a lottery ticket. The below figure visualizes the relationship between your decision on how many balls to draw from each urn (the x-axis) and the probability that a Green ball drawn from each urn awards you a lottery ticket (the y-axis). If you take your mouse cursor on the figure, it displays exact probabilities.

Here are some examples of how you read the figure.

  • Suppose you decides to draw 10 balls from Urn 1 and 90 balls from Urn 2. The decision is denoted (10,90) on the x-axis. Take your mouse cursor to where the value on the x-axis equals (10,90). Then, you will see the corresponding information that a green ball drawn from Urn 1 awards you a lottery ticket with 31.62 percent chance (blue line) and that a green ball drawn from Urn 2 awards you a lottery ticket with 10.54 percent chance (orange line).
  • Suppose you decides to draw 50 balls from Urn 1 and 50 balls from Urn 2. The decision is denoted (50,50) on the x-axis. Take your mouse cursor on the center of the figure where the value on the x-axis equals (50,50). Then, you will see the corresponding information that a green ball drawn from Urn 1 awards you a lottery ticket with 14.14 percent chance (blue line) and that a green ball drawn from Urn 2 also awards you a lottery ticket with 14.14 percent chance (orange line).
  • Suppose you decides to draw 90 balls from Urn 1 and 10 balls from Urn 2. The decision is denoted (90,10) on the x-axis. Take your mouse cursor to where the value on the x-axis equals (90,10). Then, you will see the corresponding information that a green ball drawn from Urn 1 awards you a lottery ticket with 10.54 percent chance (blue line) and that a green ball drawn from Urn 2 awards you a lottery ticket with 31.62 percent chance (orange line).




What should you do to make a good decision?

What should you do to get as many lottery tickets as possible and increase the probability of winning the prize of 200 points? In one hand, you should draw more balls from the urn that, you believe, contains more Green balls because only Green balls give you a chance to get lottery tickets. On the other hand, you do not want to draw too many balls from the urn even if you are very confident that it has far more Green balls than the other urn. The more balls are drawn from one urn, the less likely it is a Green ball drawn from that urn awards you a lottery ticket.

To help your decision-making, we provide a simulator in every decision-making screen. To use the simulator, you must report your guesses about how many Green balls you and your teammate received in Part 1. Conditional on your guesses, the simulator predicts the expected number of lottery tickets you will get for every possible decision. By "the expected number of lottery tickets you will get", we mean the average number of lottery tickets you will get if you repeatedly make the same decision infinitely many times. The simulator can inform you the optimal decision that, conditional on your reported guesses, maximizes the expected number of lottery tickets you will get and hence guarantees the highest probability of winning the prize.


Practice decision-making!

Here, you can play around with the simulator and practice your decision-making. The below is the decision-making screen you will face in the actual decision-making rounds.

There is the simulator on the top left of the decision-making screen. Note that your guesses about the number of Green balls you and your teammate received are set to be zero by default. To revise your guesses, you should enter an integer from 0-100 for each of the guesses (the minimum and the maximum numbers of Green balls one can receive are 0 and 100). Once you click "Calculate" button, you will see the corresponding simulation result. You may enter different guesses as many times as you wish. Whenever you enter new guesses, you must click "Calculate" button so that the simulator updates the simulation results accordingly.

How you interpret the simulation results? First, the x-axis represents your potential decisions. As the x-value gets closer to the right, it means more balls are drawn from Urn 1 and less balls are drawn from Urn 2. For example, the very left value on the x-axis (1,99) indicates you draw 1 ball from Urn 1 and 99 balls from Urn 2. The very right value on the x-axis (99,1) indicates you draw 99 balls from Urn 1 and 1 ball from Urn 2. Second, the y-axis represents the expected number of lottery tickets you will get, conditional on your entered guesses. Taking your mouse cursor on the simulation results displays the exact value.

As an example, let's suppose you guessed you received 40 Green balls and your teammate received 60 Green balls. Use the below practice simulator and enter 40 and 60 for your guesses about yourself and about your teammate, respectively. Then click "Calculate" button.
Once you have done this, you will see the simulator update the result. The simulation result (i.e., the blue dots) shows every pair of your potential decision and the expected number of lottery tickets you will get, conditional on your entered guess (which is in this example 40 and 60). For instance, conditional on your entered guesses, you are expected to get 2.49 lottery tickets when you draw 1 ball from Urn 1 and 99 balls from Urn 2 (the very left of the x-axis). Conditional on your entered guesses, you are expected to get 5.17 lottery tickets when you draw 99 balls from Urn 1 and 1 ball from Urn 2 (the very right of the x-axis). Also, notice that, the optimal decision that, conditional on your entered guesses, maximizes the expected number of lottery tickets you will get is tagged in red. In this example, conditional on your entered guesses, you are expected to get 5.39 lottery tickets, which is the maximum, when you draw 86 balls from Urn 1 and 14 balls from Urn 2. The simulator also announces the optimal decision at the bottom of the simulation results.

To indicate your decision, you should use the decision-slider located at the bottom left of the decision-making screen. You can submit your decision by clicking "Submit" button. The "Submit" button will appear at the bottom right of the screen in 20 seconds after each round begins. Once you have submitted your decision, you may not reverse your decision. Afterward, you will receive information about the number of lottery tickets that are actually awarded to you.

To help you keep track of your decisions over time, we also provide you with a history box located on the right of the decision-making screen.

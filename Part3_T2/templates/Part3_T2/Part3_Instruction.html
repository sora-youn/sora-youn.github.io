Part 3 consists of {{num_game_round}} rounds.

In Part 3, you are presented with two urns. Each urn contains a total of 200 balls, each of which is Green or Black.

  • Out of the 200 balls in Urn 1, there are as many as Green balls as YOU and YOUR TEAMMATE received in Part 1 . The rest of the balls are Black.
  • Out of the 200 balls in Urn 2, there are {{subcontractor}} Green balls. The rest of the balls are Black.

In every round, you are asked to draw a total of 100 balls from Urn 1 and Urn 2 with replacement. "With replacement" means that once you draw a ball from one of the two urns, you put the ball back in the urn where it was drawn before you draw another ball. Drawing with replacement makes the probabilities of drawing a Green ball (and the probabilities of drawing a Black ball) from Urn 1 and Urn 2 constant.


Here are some examples:

  • Suppose you received 40 Green balls and your teammate received 20 Green balls in Part 1.
    This means, whenever a ball is drawn from Urn 1, the ball is Green with a (40+20)/2 = 30 percent chance and Black with the remaining percent chance (100-30 = 70 percent chance). Furthermore, whenever a ball is drawn from Urn 2, the ball is Green with a {{subcontractor}}/2 = {{urn2_green}} percent chance and Black with the remaining percent chance (100-{{urn2_green}} = {{urn2_black}} percent chance).
  • Suppose you received 50 Green balls and your teammate received 50 Green balls in Part 1.
    This means, whenever a ball is drawn from Urn 1, the ball is Green with a (50+50)/2 = 50 percent chance and Black with the remaining percent chance (100-50 = 50 percent chance). Furthermore, whenever a ball is drawn from Urn 2, the ball is Green with a {{subcontractor}}/2 = {{urn2_green}} percent chance and Black with the remaining percent chance (100-{{urn2_green}} = {{urn2_black}} percent chance).
  • Suppose you received 75 Green balls and your teammate received 80 Green balls in Part 1.
    This means, whenever a ball is drawn from Urn 1, the ball is Green with a (75+80)/2 = 77.5 percent chance and Black with the remaining percent chance (100-77.5 = 22.5 percent chance). Furthermore, whenever a ball is drawn from Urn 2, the ball is Green with a {{subcontractor}}/2 = {{urn2_green}} percent chance and Black with the remaining percent chance (100-{{urn2_green}} = {{urn2_black}} percent chance).
  • These are just examples for your clarification. The chances you will face in the actual experiment depend on how many Green balls you and your teammate received in Part 1.

Remember that you and your teammate received Green balls based on the performance of the slider task in Part 1. The better YOU were at the slider task, the more Green balls you received, and hence the more Green balls there are in Urn 1. The better YOUR TEAMMATE was at the slider task, the more Green balls your teammate received, and hence the more Green balls there are in Urn 1.

In every round, you have a chance to win a prize worth {{prize}} Experimental Dollars. The chance of winning the prize increases as you earn more points. Specifically, the computer draws a random number "X" from the interval [0,{{max_lt}}] at the end of every round. Any number within the interval [0,{{max_lt}}] is equally likely to be selected. If the points you earned is greater than or equal to "X", you win the prize. If the points you earned is less than "X", you receive nothing.


Here are some examples:

  • If you've earned 0 points, there is no chance that you win the prize (0/2500).
  • If you've earned 100 points, you win the prize with a 4 percent chance (100/2500).
  • If you've earned 1000 points, you win the prize with a 40 percent chance (1000/2500).
  • If you've earned 2000 points, you win the prize with a 80 percent chance (2000/2500).
  • If you've earned 2500 points or more than 2500 points, you win the prize with a 100 percent chance (2500/2500).

At the end of Part 3, i.e. when you finished all of {{num_game_round}} rounds, one of the earnings from these {{num_game_round}} rounds will be randomly chosen as your payment from Part 3.

How do you earn points?

There are two factors that determine your points:

(1) How many Green balls are drawn:

Once you submit your decision on how many balls to draw from each urn, the computer draws 100 balls accordingly. If a drawn ball is Green, the Green ball awards you a positive number of points. If a drawn ball is Black, the Black ball awards you 0 points.
Therefore, you will earn more points as you get to draw more Green balls.

(2) How many balls you decide to draw from each urn:

How many points do you earn for each of the drawn Green balls? This depends on how many balls you have drawn from the two urns. The more balls you have drawn from one urn, the fewer points you will receive from every Green ball drawn from that urn. The fewer balls you have drawn from one urn, the more points you will receive from every Green ball drawn from the urn. The figure below visualizes the relationship. The x-axis represents your decisions on how many balls to draw from each urn. As the x-value gets closer to the right, it means you have drawn more balls from Urn 1 and have drawn fewer balls from Urn 2. For example, the very left value on the x-axis (1,99) indicates you have drawn 1 ball from Urn 1 and 99 balls from Urn 2. The very right value on the x-axis (99,1) indicates you have drawn 99 balls from Urn 1 and 1 ball from Urn 2. The y-axis represents the number of points you will earn for every Green ball drawn from each urn. If you take your mouse cursor on the figure, it displays exact values.

Here are some examples of how you read the figure.

  • Suppose you decide to draw 10 balls from Urn 1 and 90 balls from Urn 2. The decision is denoted (10,90) on the x-axis. Take your mouse cursor to where the value on the x-axis equals (10,90). Then, you will see the corresponding information that every green ball drawn from Urn 1 awards you 31.62 points (blue line) and that every green ball drawn from Urn 2 awards you 10.54 points (orange line).
  • Suppose you decide to draw 50 balls from Urn 1 and 50 balls from Urn 2. The decision is denoted (50,50) on the x-axis. Take your mouse cursor to the center of the figure where the value on the x-axis equals (50,50). Then, you will see the corresponding information that every green ball drawn from Urn 1 awards you 14.14 points (blue line) and that every green ball drawn from Urn 2 also awards you 14.14 points (orange line).
  • Suppose you decide to draw 90 balls from Urn 1 and 10 balls from Urn 2. The decision is denoted (90,10) on the x-axis. Take your mouse cursor to where the value on the x-axis equals (90,10). Then, you will see the corresponding information that every green ball drawn from Urn 1 awards you 10.54 points (blue line) and that every green ball drawn from Urn 2 awards you 31.62 points (orange line).

What should you do to make a good decision?

What should you do to earn as many points as possible and increase the probability of winning the prize of 200 Experimental Dollars? On one hand, you should draw more balls from the urn that, you believe, contains more Green balls because you will earn more points as you get to draw more Green balls. On the other hand, you do not want to draw too many balls from the urn even if you are very confident that it has far more Green balls than the other urn. The more balls are drawn from one urn, the fewer points you will earn for every Green ball drawn from that urn.

To help your decision-making, we provide a simulator in every decision-making screen. To use the simulator, you must report your guesses about how many Green balls you and your teammate received in Part 1. Conditional on your guesses, the simulator predicts the expected number of points you will earn for every possible decision. By "the expected number of points", we mean the average number of points you will earn if you repeatedly make the same decision infinitely many times. The simulator can inform you the optimal decision that, conditional on your reported guesses, maximizes the expected number of points you will earn and hence guarantees the highest probability of winning the prize.


Practice decision-making!

Here, you can play around with the simulator and practice your decision-making. The below is the decision-making screen you will face in the actual decision-making rounds.

There is the simulator on the top left of the decision-making screen. Note that your guesses about the number of Green balls you and your teammate received are set to be zero by default. To revise your guesses, you should enter an integer from 0-100 for each of the guesses (the minimum and the maximum numbers of Green balls one can receive are 0 and 100). Once you click the "Calculate" button, you will see the corresponding simulation result. You may enter different guesses as many times as you wish. Whenever you enter new guesses, you must click the "Calculate" button so that the simulator updates the simulation results accordingly.

How do you interpret the simulation results? First, the x-axis represents your potential decisions. As the x-value gets closer to the right, it means more balls are drawn from Urn 1 and fewer balls are drawn from Urn 2. For example, the very left value on the x-axis (1,99) indicates you draw 1 ball from Urn 1 and 99 balls from Urn 2. The very right value on the x-axis (99,1) indicates you draw 99 balls from Urn 1 and 1 ball from Urn 2. Second, the y-axis represents the expected number of points you will earn, conditional on your entered guesses. Taking your mouse cursor on the simulation results displays the exact values.
